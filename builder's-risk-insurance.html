Builder's Risk Insurance: Essential Protection for Construction Projects

Key Facts: Builder's Risk Insurance (also called Contractor's All Risk or CAR insurance) provides temporary coverage for buildings under construction against fire, theft, vandalism and other perils. Policies typically last 3-12 months and cover materials, fixtures and equipment on-site.

Understanding Builder's Risk Insurance

Builder's risk insurance is a specialized form of property insurance that protects construction projects from ground-breaking through completion. Unlike standard property insurance which covers finished buildings, builder's risk addresses the unique vulnerabilities of structures during construction when they are most exposed to damage.

The coverage is technically known as "Course of Construction" (COC) insurance in some regions. It serves three primary functions:

  • Protects the owner's financial interest in the project
  • Covers materials and equipment before installation
  • Provides peace of mind for all stakeholders

Legal Foundations

The need for builder's risk insurance stems from common law principles:

  • Improvement doctrine: Any construction becomes part of the landowner's property
  • Insurable interest: Contractors, lenders and owners all have financial stakes
  • Risk allocation: Determines responsibility for losses during construction

Coverage Details and Policy Structure

Builder's risk policies are highly customizable but typically include:

Standard Coverages

Coverage Type Description Limits
Building Materials Lumber, concrete, fixtures, etc. Full project value
Equipment Tools and machinery on-site Often sublimited
Soft Costs Architect fees, permits, interest 10-25% of hard costs
Debris Removal Cleanup after covered loss Included in limit

Common Policy Features

  • Duration: Typically 3-12 months (extensions available)
  • Deductibles: Usually 1-5% of project value
  • Reporting: Monthly updates often required
  • Coinsurance: Most policies require 100% coverage

What Builder's Risk Typically Covers:

  • Fire, lightning and explosion
  • Windstorms and hail
  • Theft and vandalism
  • Vehicle/aircraft damage
  • Water damage (non-flood)
  • Collapse during construction

Exclusions and Limitations

Understanding what builder's risk doesn't cover is equally important:

Standard Exclusions

  • Earth movement: Earthquakes, sinkholes (available by endorsement)
  • Flood: Surface water, storm surge (separate policy needed)
  • War/terrorism: May require special endorsement
  • Faulty workmanship: Poor quality work isn't covered
  • Employee theft: Typically excluded

Common Limitations

  • No coverage after project completion
  • No liability coverage (needs separate CGL policy)
  • No coverage for tools left unattended
  • Exclusions for certain high-risk materials

Policy Types and Purchasing Options

Builder's risk insurance comes in several forms:

Standard Policy Types

Policy Type Best For Key Features
Owner-Controlled Large commercial projects Simpler claims, broader coverage
Contractor-Controlled Residential construction Contractor manages policy
Wrap-Up Major developments Covers all parties

Purchasing Considerations

When buying builder's risk insurance:

  1. Determine project value accurately
  2. Identify all interested parties
  3. Choose appropriate policy duration
  4. Review exclusions carefully
  5. Compare multiple quotes

Case Study: High-Rise Construction Fire

A $50 million high-rise project suffered $8 million in damage from an electrical fire. The builder's risk policy covered:

  • $6.2 million in structural damage
  • $1.1 million in materials stored on-site
  • $700,000 in soft costs for delays

The claim was paid within 90 days, allowing construction to resume with minimal financial impact.

Claims Process and Risk Management

Filing a builder's risk claim requires specific documentation:

Claims Documentation

  • Original construction contracts
  • Proof of loss documentation
  • Progress reports and schedules
  • Photographic evidence
  • Material invoices

Risk Mitigation Strategies

  • Implement strict site security
  • Maintain proper fire prevention
  • Store materials securely
  • Monitor weather risks
  • Document all phases of construction

Industry Trends and Future Developments

The builder's risk market is evolving with:

  • Parametric policies: Automated payouts for specific triggers
  • Drone inspections: Improved risk assessment
  • Modular construction: New coverage approaches
  • Climate change: Increased weather-related risks
