Bill Clinton: America's 42nd President

Presidency: January 20, 1993 - January 20, 2001

Preceded by: George H.W. Bush

Succeeded by: George W. Bush

Born: William Jefferson Blythe III, August 19, 1946 (age 77), Hope, Arkansas

Political Party: Democratic

Spouse: Hillary Rodham Clinton (m. 1975)

Children: Chelsea Clinton

Early Life and Education

William Jefferson Blythe III was born on August 19, 1946, in Hope, Arkansas. His father, William Jefferson Blythe Jr., died in a car accident three months before his birth. His mother, Virginia Dell Cassidy, later married Roger Clinton Sr., whose surname young Bill would eventually adopt.

Clinton's early years were marked by financial struggles and family instability, but he excelled academically. Key educational milestones include:

  • Hot Springs High School: Distinguished himself as a saxophone player and student leader
  • Georgetown University: Earned a Bachelor of Science in Foreign Service (1968) while working for Senator J. William Fulbright
  • Oxford University: Rhodes Scholar at University College (1968-1970)
  • Yale Law School: Earned Juris Doctor (1973), where he met Hillary Rodham

Early Political Career

1974: First Political Campaign

After law school, Clinton returned to Arkansas and ran for the U.S. House of Representatives. Though he lost to incumbent Republican John Paul Hammerschmidt, the campaign established his political reputation.

1976-1978: Arkansas Attorney General

Elected at age 30, Clinton focused on consumer protection and utility rate reforms.

1978: Youngest Governor in Nation

At 32, Clinton became the youngest U.S. governor when elected Arkansas's chief executive. His first term (1979-1981) was marked by controversial policies that led to his defeat for re-election.

1983-1992: Arkansas Comeback

After winning back the governorship, Clinton served five terms, implementing education reforms and economic development programs that gained national attention.

Road to the Presidency

Clinton's successful governorship positioned him as a leader of the Democratic Party's moderate "New Democrat" faction. His 1992 presidential campaign overcame numerous obstacles:

  • Primary Challenges: Defeated established Democrats including Jerry Brown and Paul Tsongas
  • "Comeback Kid": Survived Gennifer Flowers scandal and draft controversy
  • Economic Focus: Campaign mantra "It's the economy, stupid" targeted recession-weary voters
  • Third-Party Factor: Benefited from Ross Perot's independent candidacy splitting Republican votes

Presidency (1993-2001)

Clinton's two terms were marked by economic prosperity, policy innovations, and personal scandals.

First Term (1993-1997)

Key achievements and challenges:

  • Economic Policy: Passed deficit reduction package leading to budget surpluses
  • NAFTA: Implemented North American Free Trade Agreement despite opposition
  • Health Care Reform: Hillary Clinton-led task force proposal failed in Congress
  • Crime Bill: Signed Violent Crime Control and Law Enforcement Act
  • Midterm Loss: Republicans gained Congress in 1994 "Republican Revolution"

Second Term (1997-2001)

Notable developments:

  • Economic Boom: Presided over longest peacetime expansion in U.S. history
  • Welfare Reform: Signed Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Act
  • Balanced Budget: Achieved first surplus since 1969
  • Foreign Policy: Brokered Northern Ireland peace; NATO intervention in Kosovo
  • Impeachment: Acquitted in Senate trial after House impeachment for perjury regarding Monica Lewinsky affair

Major Domestic Policies

Economic Achievements

  • Created 22 million new jobs
  • Lowest unemployment since 1960s (4.0%)
  • Highest homeownership in U.S. history
  • Converted $290 billion deficit to $236 billion surplus

Social Policies

  • Family and Medical Leave Act (1993)
  • Brady Bill gun control legislation
  • Don't Ask, Don't Tell military policy
  • Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP)

Foreign Policy

Clinton's foreign policy focused on economic globalization and humanitarian intervention:

  • Middle East: Facilitated Oslo Accords between Israel and PLO
  • Europe: NATO expansion into Eastern Europe; Kosovo intervention
  • Trade: Permanent Normal Trade Relations with China; WTO establishment
  • Africa: Apologized for U.S. inaction during Rwanda genocide

Scandals and Controversies

Clinton's presidency was marked by several major controversies:

  • Whitewater: Real estate investment investigation
  • Lewinsky Affair: Led to impeachment for perjury and obstruction
  • Travelgate: White House Travel Office firings
  • Pardons: Controversial last-minute pardons including Marc Rich

Post-Presidency

Since leaving office, Clinton has remained active in public life:

  • Clinton Foundation: Global initiative addressing health, economic, and climate issues
  • Humanitarian Work: Tsunami and earthquake relief efforts
  • Political Role: Campaigning for Democratic candidates including Hillary Clinton's presidential bids
  • Author: Published bestselling memoir "My Life" (2004)

Legacy and Historical Rankings

Historians generally rate Clinton as an above-average president:

  • Recognized for economic management and political skills
  • Criticized for personal scandals undermining moral authority
  • Ranks in top third of presidents in most scholarly surveys
  • Credited with revitalizing Democratic Party after Reagan era

Personal Life

Married to Hillary Rodham Clinton since 1975, they are the only presidential couple to both serve as U.S. Senators and run for president. Their daughter Chelsea Clinton has become a public figure in her own right.

Clinton has faced serious health challenges including quadruple bypass surgery in 2004 but remains active in public life at age 77.
