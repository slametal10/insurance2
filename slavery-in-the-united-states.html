Slavery in the United States: 1619-1865

Key Facts: Between 1619 and 1865, approximately 10.7 million Africans survived the Middle Passage to the Americas, with about 388,000 arriving in what became the United States. By 1860, the enslaved population had grown to nearly 4 million, valued at over $3 billion - more than all railroads and factories combined.

Origins and Colonial Period (1619-1775)

The institution of slavery in British North America began when approximately 20 enslaved Africans arrived at Point Comfort, Virginia in 1619 aboard an English privateer ship. This marked the beginning of a system that would shape American society for nearly 250 years.

Early Development

  • 1619: First Africans arrive in Virginia
  • 1641: Massachusetts becomes first colony to legalize slavery
  • 1662: Virginia passes law making slavery hereditary
  • 1705: Virginia Slave Codes establish comprehensive legal framework

The Triangular Trade

The transatlantic slave trade operated as a three-part system:

  1. European goods traded for African captives
  2. Middle Passage transported slaves to Americas
  3. American products (cotton, tobacco, sugar) shipped to Europe

This system enriched port cities like Boston, New York, and Charleston while devastating African societies.

Revolutionary Era and Constitution (1775-1789)

The American Revolution created contradictions between ideals of liberty and the reality of slavery:

Key Developments

Year Event Impact
1775 Lord Dunmore's Proclamation Offered freedom to slaves joining British
1777 Vermont bans slavery First abolition in North America
1780 Pennsylvania Gradual Abolition Model for Northern states
1787 Northwest Ordinance Banned slavery in new territories
"We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal..." - Declaration of Independence, 1776 (written by slaveholder Thomas Jefferson)

Antebellum Period and Expansion (1790-1860)

The invention of the cotton gin in 1793 reinvigorated slavery, leading to its expansion westward:

Economic Impact

  • Cotton became America's leading export
  • By 1860, Southern slaves produced 75% of world's cotton
  • Enslaved population grew from 700,000 (1790) to nearly 4 million (1860)
  • "Second Middle Passage" forcibly moved 1 million slaves westward
1808 - International slave trade banned (but smuggling continued)
1820 - Missouri Compromise attempts to balance slave/free states
1850 - Fugitive Slave Act requires Northern cooperation
1857 - Dred Scott decision denies citizenship to African Americans

Life Under Slavery

The experience of enslaved people varied by region and labor type:

Types of Labor

Work Type Description Conditions
Field Hands Agricultural labor on plantations Dawn to dusk work, brutal conditions
Domestic Servants Household work in owner's home Closer supervision but constant availability
Skilled Labor Carpenters, blacksmiths, etc. Some mobility, often hired out

Resistance and Rebellion

Enslaved people resisted through various means:

  • Day-to-day resistance: Work slowdowns, feigned illness
  • Escape: Underground Railroad helped ~100,000 escape
  • Rebellions: Nat Turner (1831), Denmark Vesey (1822)
  • Cultural preservation: Music, religion, family ties

Civil War and Emancipation (1861-1865)

The Civil War began as a conflict over union but became a war to end slavery:

Key Events

  • 1861: Contraband policy begins protecting escapees
  • 1863: Emancipation Proclamation frees slaves in rebel states
  • 1865: 13th Amendment abolishes slavery nationwide
  • 1865: Freedmen's Bureau established to assist former slaves

Legacy: While legal slavery ended in 1865, its impacts persisted through Jim Crow laws, sharecropping, racial terrorism, and systemic discrimination whose effects continue to shape American society today.
